By Ed Cotton · Inverness Consulting

Scroll to begin ↓

In a world where creativity is under pressure, I wanted to look out into the world at a variety of creative practices across different disciplines and learn about how they thought, how they worked, and what they made.

I wanted to identify some core themes and ideas that crossed boundaries. I wanted to know how they might stand up to AI.

I wanted to consider the idea of how they could unite in the name of human craft, human scale, and process, and what this might look like.

What follows is a study of 51 practices across 9 disciplines, 15 countries, and 6 decades. It became an argument for friction.

The thesis

The friction is the point

AI's entire value proposition is the elimination of friction — seamless, instant, frictionless generation. Every practice in this document introduces friction deliberately. The resistance is the work.

This is a study of 51 creative practices across 9 disciplines, 15 countries, and 6 decades. Each one has chosen friction over frictionlessness — slowness over speed, materials over screens, refusal over compliance, the journey over the output. Together, they reveal a structural argument about what human creativity is and why it cannot be generated.

The argument

The journey is the value.
The output is the receipt.

AI skips straight to the output. It generates the residue of a creative process without undergoing the process. The bread looks like bread. The strategy reads like a strategy. But nothing has been through anything.

"Baking bread is not about controlling the process; it's about guiding the fermentation. You are there to act as a catalyst for what the dough naturally wants to do." Chad Robertson, Tartine

Robertson doesn't make bread. He guides a fermentation — a negotiation between a human, a living culture, and the specific microbiome of the room. The bread is what's left at the end of that negotiation. If you just eat the bread, you're eating the receipt.

"One artisan cannot finish the work of another artisan." Matthieu Blazy, Chanel (formerly Bottega Veneta)

Blazy doesn't make leather that looks like denim. He runs a workshop where eight different pairs of hands each make independent judgements about the piece passing through them. The garment is the fossil record of those decisions. AI can produce the surface. It cannot produce the sequence of separate human judgements.

"Emoticons don't have wrinkles. Authored craft does." Luna Maurer & Roel Wouters, Moniker

The wrinkles are the evidence. The proof that a specific person, in a specific place, over a specific period of time, made choices that accumulated into something that could not have been produced any other way. AI generates without wrinkles. Without history. Without scars. Pristine — and therefore empty.

The layers

Three layers of friction

Every practice operates across three nested layers. The cascade runs from inside to outside — and back again:

Layer 1
The Practitioner — who they are, how they prepare, how long they take. Biographical (51%), Spiritual (27%), Temporal (14%).
Layer 2
The Process — what materials they use, how they research, what constraints they accept. Material and Institutional tied at 25% each.
Layer 3
The Output — how the work communicates, reveals itself, ages. Aesthetic (39%), Perceptual (37%).

The strongest cascade in the data: Spiritual practitioners default to Material process friction 50% of the time. The meditation trains the eye. The eye reads the stone. The stone shapes the building. The building weathers. And Material process friction cascades to Aesthetic output 77% of the time.

AI mimics Layer 3 — the surface, the aesthetic, the style. It cannot reproduce Layers 1 and 2 — the meditation, the biography, the years of material engagement. The authored object is the outermost layer of a friction that begins inside the maker.

The vulnerability

What protects. What doesn't.

Each practice was scored on AI vulnerability based on four dimensions: friction mode, practitioner layer, process layer, and output layer. Lower score = more resistant to AI replication.

65

By discipline: Food (avg 15) and Architecture (avg 22.5) are the most AI-resistant. Technology (avg 51.9) and Brand & Identity (avg 48.2) are the most vulnerable. The further from the physical, the closer to AI replicability.

But this scoring has a blind spot. It measures the output's replicability, not the journey's. Collins's actual product is not a strategy document — it's a client organisation transformed by the process of working with his team. That transformation has a vulnerability score of approximately zero.

The threat

Good enough is the enemy

The threat isn't that AI produces bad work. The threat is that it produces acceptable work. And acceptable, at scale, for free, eliminates the encounter with extraordinary.

If you've never tasted bread made from a living culture, supermarket bread is fine. If you've never walked through Therme Vals, a rendered spa is fine. If you've never worn clothes cut by someone who understands how fabric falls on a moving body, algorithmically fitted garments are fine.

Good enough isn't a conscious choice. It's the absence of the encounter that would have made the choice obvious.

"The world is being filled with too much work that isn't interesting enough." Lynsey Atkin, Baby Teeth

AI generates. Authored craft accumulates. AI's thousandth output is identical to its first. A human practitioner's thousandth repetition is richer — scarred, deepened, redirected by everything that happened along the way. History is the one thing that cannot be generated.

The skills

What AI cannot replicate

Five human capacities, ranked by AI resistance:

1. Sustained attention to physical materials. Zumthor's months of walking. Robertson's decades with fermentation. Nghia feeling each bamboo culm. The ability to listen to resistance and adjust. AI processes information. These practitioners process material. The difference: material pushes back.

2. Productive disagreement. The triad — three different perspectives held in tension. Baby Teeth, Torrisi, Formafantasma, Porto Rocha. AI resolves tension. Humans hold it long enough for something unexpected to emerge.

3. Strategic refusal. The capacity for no. Are.na refuses algorithms. Philo refuses advertising. Porto Rocha refuses free pitches. AI is architecturally incapable of unprompted refusal. Every prompt gets a response.

4. Biographical accumulation. The twenty-year silence. The immigrant's double vision. The decades of walking the same hills. A human's thousandth repetition is richer. AI's is identical.

5. Institutional knowledge. The design system, the brand manual, the methodology. The most valued by organisations — and the most vulnerable to AI, because institutions exist to make tacit knowledge explicit. Explicit knowledge is training data.

The skills most valued by organisations are the skills most vulnerable to AI. The skills most resistant to AI are the ones organisations have spent decades trying to systematise out of existence.

The parallel

Why creativity must come together

The industrial revolution replaced hands. AI replaces minds. That's a category difference.

The Arts and Crafts movement responded by retreating into preciousness — beautiful things only the wealthy could afford. It failed. The Bauhaus responded by engaging across disciplines — master the machine, direct it. It succeeded. But AI requires a third response, because AI isn't dumb. You cannot simply direct it.

The human contribution needs to be something deeper than direction. Something AI cannot provide even when it can generate, decide, and optimise on its own.

That something is the journey.

The journey argument cannot be made from within a single discipline. A baker saying "fermentation matters more than the loaf" sounds like special pleading. But when a baker, an architect, a strategist, a fashion designer, a publisher, and a software designer all say it at once — the argument changes category. It becomes a structural claim about what human creativity is.

"Materials are heavy and must therefore be local. But ideas, being light, can travel." Jan Boelen, Atelier LUMA

The disciplines don't need to merge. They need to recognise each other. To see that the inside-precedes-the-outside principle, the community-precedes-brand principle, the material-speaks principle, and the refusal-as-authorship principle are the same principle in different materials. And to say so together.

The call

The friction is the journey.
The journey is the transformation.
The transformation is the value.

The output is just the receipt. And the receipt, no matter how beautifully generated, cannot substitute for the dinner.

"Craft is within yourself, not necessarily outside." Bijoy Jain, Studio Mumbai
"Design is not what we make. Design is what we make possible." Brian Collins
"I always make a point of choosing the difficult way." Oki Sato, Nendo
"Everyone, rich or poor, deserves a shelter for the soul." Samuel Mockbee, Rural Studio

51 practices. 9 disciplines. 15 countries. One shared principle: the resistance is the work. The friction produces the depth. The journey forms the output, forms the people, and forms the relationships. Without skin in the game there is no passion. Without passion nothing convinces. Without the work you don't get to something worth fighting for.

It is time for the disciplines to see each other. To recognise the shared principle. And to say so, together, as loudly as the machines.
