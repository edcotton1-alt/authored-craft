The Stories

Six practices examined in depth — one story for each friction that shapes how authored craft survives in an age of generation.

Dissatisfaction
Baby Teeth

Lynsey Atkin looked at the creative industry in 2025 and said out loud what everyone was thinking: the world is being filled with too much work that isn't interesting enough.

She didn't write a LinkedIn post about it. She quit. She and two partners — a producer and a brand strategist — built a company that doesn't sell creative services at all. Baby Teeth owns the ideas. Not the executions, not the campaigns, not the decks. The ideas. When AI made execution cheap, they realised the only thing left worth owning was the thing AI couldn't generate: conviction about what to make and why.

"The world is being filled with too much work that isn't interesting enough." Lynsey Atkin — Baby Teeth

Three founders. Three genuinely different skills. Creative direction, production, brand partnerships. None of them could do what Baby Teeth does alone. They didn't start a new agency. They killed the agency model and built something that doesn't have a name yet.

Between them: BAFTAs, Cannes Lions, D&AD, work archived at MoMA. And they walked away from all the structures that produced those credentials because the structures were producing too much work that wasn't interesting enough.

Rules
Teenage Engineering

Jesper Kouthoofd limits himself to 40 RAL colours. That's it. That's the design system. 40 colours, simple geometric shapes, and a rule that orange means recording.

He didn't want to start a business. Handling employees, having meetings — super-boring. He couldn't be employed anywhere else. He co-founded ACNE, left because the culture changed, directed commercials, collected Lamborghinis, then met some programmer friends in a warehouse. The OP-1 synthesiser came out of that warehouse in 2010. It's still the reference product in its category fifteen years later.

"I set up restrictions when I design. I stick to rules, because then I can become free again." Jesper Kouthoofd — Teenage Engineering

When he designs a product, it's like making a film. Each one tells a story. The Pocket Operator costs $59 and sounds like nothing else because it was designed inside the same 40-colour box that produced the $2,000 OP-1. IKEA hired him. Nothing hired him. Panic hired him. SFMOMA exhibited him. None of them got a Teenage Engineering that looked like anything other than Teenage Engineering, because the rules don't change for clients.

AI operates in infinite possibility space. Every style, every colour, every form. Kouthoofd operates in 40 colours. His constraint is his freedom. AI's freedom is its problem.
Partners
Torrisi / Major Food Group

You can't make a team of jump shooters. Mario Carbone said that, and he meant it structurally.

Rich Torrisi does R&D — menu conceptualisation, culinary standards across every restaurant, training. Mario Carbone does creative direction — music, wardrobe, china, glassware, graphic design, front-of-house culture. Jeff Zalaznick does business development, partnerships, expansion.

Three people. Three genuinely different skills. They fight for a common goal but they cannot do each other's jobs. The chef cannot choose the music. The creative director cannot develop the menu. The business lead cannot curate the art. And the restaurants — 50+ venues across 15 cities — are the product of that structured disagreement.

"We all fight for a common goal, but you have to have different skill sets." Jeff Zalaznick — Torrisi / Major Food Group

Each venue is a collaboration with a different designer and a different city. Vito Schnabel curates the art. Ken Fulk designs the interiors. Renzo Piano designed Santina under the High Line. Annabelle Selldorf restored the Four Seasons space. The expansion doesn't template. It partners. Each restaurant is the product of a specific argument between specific people in a specific place.

AI can generate a restaurant concept. It cannot generate the argument between a chef who wants to honour his grandmother's recipe, a creative director who wants Julian Schnabel paintings on the walls, and a business strategist who wants it to work in Mayfair.
Systems
Matthieu Blazy / Chanel

Matthieu Blazy makes leather that looks like denim. That's what the headlines say. What actually happens is more extraordinary than any headline can contain.

A piece enters the atelier in Montebello. It passes through eight different pairs of hands. Each artisan reads what the previous one did and responds to it. Not from a blueprint. From the material. One artisan cannot finish the work of another artisan — Blazy's own words, and the most potent anti-AI statement in the entire study.

"One artisan cannot finish the work of another artisan." Matthieu Blazy — Chanel

The garment that emerges is the fossil record of eight separate human judgements. No single intelligence — human or artificial — could have orchestrated the sequence of decisions that gave the piece its character. The character lives in the handoffs. In the moments where one person's touch meets another person's response. In the seams between perspectives.

The more boundaries you have, the more freedom you can find in the small things. Blazy found his system not by inventing it but by inheriting the atelier's centuries-old way of working and understanding why it produces things that nothing else can. The system is the people. The people are not interchangeable. The craft is so advanced it becomes invisible — leather that looks like denim, knit that looks like leather, surfaces so sophisticated you'd mistake them for something ordinary. That's the point. The highest craft disappears.

AI can replicate the surface. It cannot replicate the sequence of eight pairs of hands that produced it.
Work / Life
Vo Trong Nghia

Vietnam has less than one square metre of green space per person in Ho Chi Minh City. The default development model is concrete and steel. Vo Trong Nghia builds with bamboo.

Not because it's traditional. Because the material demands a quality of attention that a distracted mind cannot provide. His staff meditate for two hours every morning before they're allowed to pick up a pencil. This isn't corporate wellness. This is the practice.

"Architects must heal themselves in order to heal the world." Vo Trong Nghia — VTN Architects

42,000 bamboo culms in a single structure. Five years to harvest them. When the global architecture industry generates from a palette of steel, glass, and concrete, the architect who insists on bamboo is not making a design decision. He's making a political, ecological, and spiritual one. The bamboo is local. The knowledge of how to shape it is local. The craft of bending it without breaking it lives in the hands of workers whose skills cannot be downloaded.

The meditation came first. The buildings followed. The buildings are what happen when an architect decides that the quality of his attention matters more than the speed of his output. The attention shapes the hand. The hand shapes the bamboo. The bamboo shapes the building. The building shapes the city.

AI generates architecture from plans. Nghia generates it from breath.
Patience
Peter Zumthor

Peter Zumthor can remember the door handle of his aunt's house. It was shaped like the back of a spoon. He can hear the gravel under his feet, smell the waxed oak staircase, feel the heavy front door closing behind him.

He designs for that. Not for the eye's impression. For the body's memory. The door handle passage is from Thinking Architecture, and it is the counter-argument to every AI-generated architectural rendering on earth.

"We actually never talk about form in the office. We talk about construction, we talk about science, and we talk about feelings." Peter Zumthor — on the LACMA building

His LACMA building — the David Geffen Galleries, 900 feet long, crossing Wilshire Boulevard, 30 feet above street level — took more than 20 years from conception to its opening in April 2026. First design 2013. Major redesign 2014. Construction completed end of 2024. He began thinking about it in 2001.

19 built works in 45 years. No studio website. He declined Tom Ford's house. He discovered that stone and water have a love relationship. He said goodbye to the overworked notion that architecture has to save the world. He works in Haldenstein, a village in the Swiss canton of Graubünden, where he has been since 1979. He's not going anywhere. He's not in a hurry.

AI can render any building in seconds. It cannot take 20 years to understand why a building should exist.
